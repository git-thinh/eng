Word classes are not the same as clause elements. The Clause elements are Subject, Verb and Object. The Verb and Subject are sometimes known as the predicate when together. Word classes are in the groupings of Adjective, Verb and Noun. These then go into subgroups of: evaluative, comparative and superlative adjectives; stative, dynamic and auxiliary verbs; concrete and abstract nouns. This is why we need to differentiate between clause elements and word classes.

Word classes (noun, verb, adjective) describe what a word does in relation to other words, while clause elements (subject, object, complement) describe how the different parts of a sentence interact. As you can see demonstrated , clause elements are defined much more broadly than word classes and can be filled by a wide variety of word and phrase constructs.

In essence, sentences/clauses are constructed out of clause elements which are constructed out of individual words, as defined by word classes. They describe two different "levels" of the grammar of an utterance.
